The Solubility Pump#

The majority of carbon (~98%) in the ocean exists as Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC). The DIC pool is therefore the primary carbon reservoir in the ocean, and the chemical properties of DIC determine the ocean’s ability to store carbon. We’ll now look at how CO2 is related to DIC.

CO2 dissolves in water#

As we saw in the last lecture, CO2(g) behaves like an ideal gas and dissolves from air into water until it reaches an equilibrium with CO2(aq) concentration that is described by Henry’s Law (Eq. (7.1)):

The solubility of a gas in water depends strongly on temperature, and weakly on salinity (fig:carbon:K0). More gas will dissolve into colder, fresher water than warmer, saltier water.

The variability of gas solubility interacts with Thermohaline Circulation (THC) to elevate carbon concentrations in the deep ocean. During surface transport (e.g. along Western boundary currents like the Gulf Stream) water loses heat to the atmosphere and cools down (fig:carbon:oceanheat). This increases \(K_0\), allowing the water to hold more dissolved CO2 at a given atmospheric pCO2. The water reaches as a temperature minimum, and therefore [CO2(aq)] maximum, at its density maximum just before it sinks to the deep ocean. Once in the deep ocean that water is isolated from the atmosphere until it resurfaces somewhere along the circulation path. The downwelling of cold, CO2-rich water therefore removes carbon from the atmosphere until that water is upwelled again 100s-1000s of years later. Consequently, these upwelling zones outgas CO2 to the atmosphere as the water warms up at the surface. This cycle, driven by changes in gas solubility in water, is known as the solubility pump.

The solubulity of CO2 explains first-order patterns of CO2 uptake and release in terms of ocean circulation, but is not enough to explain the high concentration of inorganic carbon in the ocean. If we take the entire range of \(K_0\) (0.02 to 0.07) at modern atmospheric pCO2 (420 ppm), this only allows an equilibrium CO2(aq) concentration of between 8.4-33.6 μmol kg-1. Measurements of inorganic carbon in the ocean show that the actual concentration is on the order of ~2000 μmol kg-1.

CO2 reacts with water#

There is more carbon in the ocean than we would expect based on gas solubility because CO2(aq) reacts with water to form non-gaseous species that cannot exchange with the atmosphere. Once CO2(g) is dissolved to CO2(aq) it reacts with water to form a weak diprotic acid (an acid with two protons, H+) called carbonic acid. This acid dissociates to produce the Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC) species:

()#\[CO_{2(g)} \leftrightarrow \underbrace{CO_{2(aq)}}_{dissolved~gas} + H_2O \leftrightarrow \underbrace{H_2CO_3}_{carbonic~acid} \leftrightarrow \underbrace{HCO_3^-}_{bicarbonate} + H^+ \leftrightarrow \underbrace{CO_3^{2-}}_{carbonate} + 2H^+\]

Where DIC is defined as the sum of these species in seawater:

Units!

Square brackets denote the concentration of a dissolved chemical species

()#\[\begin{split}DIC &= [CO_{2(aq)}] + [H_2CO_3] + [HCO_3^-] + [CO_3^{2-}] \\ &= [CO_2^*] + [HCO_3^-] + [CO_3^{2-}]\end{split}\]

where \([CO_2^*] = [CO_{2(aq)}] + [H_2CO_3]\) because carbonic acid is functionally absent in normal seawater - as soon as CO2(aq) reacts with water the carbonic acid dissociates to form bicarbonate and a proton.

Note on CO2*…

[CO2*] is a good approximation in most cases, but be aware that the reaction of CO2(aq) with water is much slower than the subsequent dissociation reactions because it requires breaking strong C-O bonds, whereas the dissociation involves adding/removing weakly-bound protons (H+). This means that the CO2* approximation may start to break down in dynamic systems where DIC speciation is far from equilibrium with CO2(aq). This can become important when considering rapid ocean-atmosphere interactions, because CO2(aq), rather than than H2CO3, is the species that exchanges with CO2(g) in the atmosphere.

Because CO2(aq) is the only species that exchanges with CO2(g) in the atmosphere, the speciation of DIC is critical when considering the relationship between DIC and pCO2. From Eq () we see that DIC speciation will depend on the concentration of each of the DIC species and H+ in the water. The concentration of H+ is described by the pH of the water (pH = -log10[H+]), which tells us that the concentration of the carbon species in seawater is a function of pH (fig:carbon:bjerrum).

Note that at normal surface seawater pH (~8.1, fig:carbon:bjerrum), very little of the DIC is in the form of CO2(aq) - most is in the form of the non-exchangeable species bicarbonate and carbonate.

DIC Speciation#

The speciation of DIC is described by acid-base equilibria described by the equilibrium constants K1 and K2:

()#\[\begin{split}CO_{2}^* &\overset{K_1}{\leftrightarrow} HCO_3^- + H^+ \\ HCO_3^- &\overset{K_2}{\leftrightarrow} CO_3^{2-} + H^+\end{split}\]

Where K1 and K2 are defined as:

()#\[\begin{split}K_1 &= \frac{[H^+][HCO_3^-]}{[CO_2^*]} \\ K_2 &= \frac{[H^+][CO_3^{2-}]}{[HCO_3^-]}\end{split}\]

These can be combined to define the concentration of each DIC species as a function of [DIC], pH and the equilibrium constants K1 and K2:

[CO2*] derivation…

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} DIC &= [CO_2^*] + [HCO_3^-] + [CO_3^{2-}] \\ &= [CO_2^*] + [HCO_3^-] + \frac{K_2 [HCO_3^-]}{[H^+]} & \mathrm{substitute ~ [CO_3^{2-}] ~ using ~ K_2} \\ &= [CO_2^*] + \frac{K_1[CO_2^*]}{[H^+]} + \frac{K_2 K_1 [CO_2^*]}{[H^+]^2} & \mathrm{substitute ~ [HCO_3^{-}] ~ using ~ K_1} \\ &= [CO_2^*] \left( 1 + \frac{K_1}{[H^+]} + \frac{K_2 K_1}{[H^+]^2} \right) & \mathrm{separate ~ [CO_2^*]} \\ [CO_2^*] &= \frac{DIC}{1 + \frac{K_1}{[H^+]} + \frac{K_1K_2}{[H^+]^2}} & \mathrm{solve ~ for ~ [CO_2^*]} \end{align} \end{split}\]
()#\[\begin{split}[CO_2^*] &= \frac{DIC}{1 + \frac{K_1}{[H^+]} + \frac{K_1K_2}{[H^+]^2}} \\ [HCO_3^-] &= \frac{DIC}{1 + \frac{[H^+]}{K_1} + \frac{K_2}{[H^+]^2}} \\ [CO_3^{2-}] &= \frac{DIC}{1 + \frac{[H^+]}{K_2} + \frac{[H^+]^2}{K_1K_2}} \\\end{split}\]

These Ks have been carefully determined for seawater, and can be calculated as a function of temperature, salinity and pressure. At standard salinity and temperature (35, 25 °C) and 1 atmosphere pressure (surface seawater), these K and pK (-log10K) are:

Solving the DIC system…

We’ve now seen that measuring the concentration of DIC in seawater is not enough to understand ocean carbon - we also need to know the state of the carbon species in seawater. How do we determine this?

The equilibrium equations in () define a system of two equations with four unknowns (\([CO_2^*], [HCO_3^-], [CO_3^{2-}] and [H^+]\)). To determine the concentration of an individual DIC species we therefore need to know two of these variables.

In reality only one of these variables, pH, can be practically measured in seawater, which presents a problem. There are, however, two other properties that can be readily measured related to ocean carbon: DIC contencentration, and Total Alkalinity \((TA = 2 [CO_3^{2-}] + [HCO_3^-] + [minor~bases] + [OH^-] - [H^+])\), which is the sum of the charge of weak bases in seawater (don’t worry about this - it’s far beyond the scope of this course!). We now have four equations and six unknowns, but three of those unknowns are relatively easy to measure in seawater.

pCO2, pH and DIC#

From the equations and K values above, we can calculate that for a typical ocean pH of ~8.1, only ~0.5% of DIC exists as CO2* at ‘standard’ conditions. This shows why the concentration of DIC in the ocean is so much higher than predicted by the concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere. However, this simple calculation implies that pH and DIC speciation are independent of each other, which is not the case.

In fig:carbon:bjerrum we show the concentration of the DIC species changing solely as a function of water pH, but this is only true for a fixed DIC concentration. You can see from ()-() that the concentration of each DIC species and [H+] (and therefore pH) exist in an equilibrium. This means that if any component of the system is changed, all parts of the system will adjust to reach a new equilibrium state.

This has two important consequences for our understanding of ocean carbon:

1. pCO2 and pH#

Atmospheric pCO2 and ocean pH are coupled - increasing pCO2 will decrease pH in a process known as Ocean Acidification. This follows directly from (), where we see that the immediate dissociation of carbonic acid releases a proton into the ocean. The majority of these protons will react with CO32- to form HCO3-, but the new equilibrium reached will be at a lower pH, with consequently different DIC speciation (towards the left in fig:carbon:bjerrum).

Conversely, any process that independently alters the pH of the ocean will change the speciation of DIC, allowing the surface ocean to take up or release CO2. This becomes important when considering biological processes, which we will discuss in the next two sections.

2. pCO2 and DIC#

DIC does not increase with [CO2] in a 1:1 relationship. fig:carbon:pCO2-DIC shows the change in DIC concentration and speciation as pCO2 increases. Here, the increase in [CO2] is set entirely by Henry’s Law, but the total increase in DIC concentration includes the increase in non-exchangeable carbon species that occurs as that CO2 reacts with water in the ocean. This causes the total increase in DIC per unit pCO2 to be much greater than the increase in [CO2*] alone, amplifying the effect of solubility pump that is driven by changes in CO2 solubility.

Modelling the Solubility Pump#

To include the solubility pump in our model, we add:

  • A box to represent the atmosphere.

  • The concentration of DIC.

  • The speciation of DIC.

  • The exchange of CO2 between the atmosphere and each surface box.

Atmospheric Carbon#

We will model the atmosphere as a single, well-mixed box that overlies both surface ocean boxes. This is an OK approximation because the processes of atmospheric mixing are much faster than the processes of ocean mixing. Our atmosphere will have a pCO2, which is related to the number of moles of CO2 in the atmosphere by:

()#\[N_{CO2} = pCO_2~N_{air}\times 10^{-6}\]

Where \(N_{air}\) is the total number of moles of air in the atmosphere and \(pCO_2\) is in ppm. We can also calculate the mass of carbon in the atmosphere:

()#\[m_{CO2} = N_{CO2} 12\]

For an initial pCO2 of 400 ppm with Nair of 1.773x1020, this yields a totoal mass of 851 GtC in out model atmosphere.

Ocean-Atmosphere Exchange#

We will model exchange in the atmosphere by allowing CO2 in each surface box to equilibrate with the overlying atmosphere with a characteristic timescale, \(\tau^{CO2}\), which defines how quickly CO2 the atmosphere and the surface box will equilibrate (Fig. 7.6). This allows us to calculate the number of moles of C moving from the atmosphere into one of the surface boxes as:

()#\[\dv{DIC_{CO2,i}}{t} = - \frac{1}{\tau^{CO2}_i} V_i \left([CO_2]_i - \underbrace{K_{0,i} ~ pCO_2}_{\mathrm{\mu mol~kg^{-1}}} \right)\]

Where \(i\) is either of the surface boxes (\(H, L\)). Note that we have to be a bit careful with units here, as \(K_0 pCO_2\) will return units of \(\mu mol~kg^{-1}\) if \(pCO_2\) is given in ppm - this has to be in the same units as \([CO_2]_i\).

Conversely, the change in \(pCO_2\) of the atmosphere can be calculated as:

()#\[\dv{pCO_2}{t} = \sum_{i=L,H} \left(\frac{1}{\tau^{CO2}_i} V_i \left([CO_2]_i - K_{0,i} ~ pCO_2 \right) \right) \times 10^{6} / N_{air}\]

To calculate this flux accurately, we need to know both the concentration of DIC and the state of DIC speciation to determine \([CO_2]_i\). We can model DIC just like any other conservative tracer (7.2), but keeping track of speciation is a bit more complicated.

DIC Speciation#

Above we calculated DIC speciation as a function of pH and the speciation constants \(K_1\) and \(K_2\), so can we use pH to model DIC speciation? Unfortunately, pH is not a conservative quantity. The \(K\) values for DIC speciation are sensitive to temperature, salinity and pressure, which also make pH sensitive to these parameters. This allows pH to change based on the physical properties of the box, so the total amount of \(H^+\) in the entire model is not constant. To track DIC speciation, we need a conservative quantity that, along with DIC, can be used to calculate the concentration of CO2 in each surface box.

This conservative quantity is Total Alkalinity (TA). This is a conceptually tricky quantity that is beyond the scope of this course to understand. For our purposes, it is sufficient to know that TA is conservative, and that TA and DIC together can be used to calculate aqueous carbon speciation. In practicals, you will use a python package for doing these calculations.

No but what actually is TA?!

OK… you asked for it. TA is a difficult concept. For a chemist, TA is “a measure of the excess bases (proton acceptors) over acids (proton donors), and is operationally defined by the titration with H+ of all weak bases present in the solution” (Sarmiento & Gruber, 2006). For a geochemist, “TA is equal to the charge difference between conservative cations and anions”, so is a measure of the charge imbalance of seawater. It can also be thought of as the ‘buffering capacity’ of water, as higher alkalinity water has more proton acceptors so can better resist changes in pH.

Functionally, it is approximately defined as: \( TA = 2[CO_3^{2-}] + [HCO_3^-] + [B(OH)_4^-] + [minor~bases] + [OH^-] - [H^+] \)

If you really want to know the details, take a look at section 1.2 of Zeebe & Wolf-Gladrow (2001)… but you really do not need to know this for QES.

As no processes in our model currently modify TA (they will soon), we can model TA using the equations for conservative tracer transport given in (7.2).

DIC Concentration#

Now we have a way to keep track of DIC speciation, we can calculate the concentration of CO2 in each surface box as:

\[[CO_2]_i = f(DIC_i, TA_i, T_i, S_i)\]

This allows us to model DIC as a conservative tracer (7.2), with additional terms for the exchange of CO2 with the atmosphere in the surface boxes:

()#\[\begin{split}\dv{DIC_L}{t} &= [\mathrm{transport}] - \frac{1}{\tau^{CO2}_L} ([CO_2]_L - K_{0,L} ~ pCO_2 ) \\ \dv{DIC_H}{t} &= [\mathrm{transport}] - \frac{1}{\tau^{CO2}_H} ([CO_2]_H - K_{0,H} ~ pCO_2 ) \\ \dv{DIC_D}{t} &= [\mathrm{transport}]\end{split}\]

where \([\mathrm{transport}]\) represents the conservative transport terms from (7.2). Note that the deep box is only affected by transport terms because it does not exchange CO2 with the atmosphere.
